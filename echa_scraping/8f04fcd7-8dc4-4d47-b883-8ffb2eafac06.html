    Additional information - workers

    The only hazard identified is eye irritation:

    The registered substance is classified as Eye irritant 2A, H319. It is not possible to derive a DNEL based on this type of effect. According to the ECHA guidance on Information Requirements and Chemical Safety Assessment, Part E, Table E. 3-1 (Version 3.0 November 2016), the substance is categorized in the ‘low hazard’ band for eye irritation and a qualitative risk characterisation should be performed for this endpoint. In order to guarantee ‘adequately control of risks’, it is necessary to stipulate risk management measures that prevent exposure that will cause eye irritation.

     

     

    Studies where test item related adverse effects were observed:

    For derivation of DNELs, all available hazard information needs to be evaluated. The first step for DNEL derivation [1] is to gather typical dose descriptors (e.g. N(L)OAEL, BMD, LD50, LC50, T25, BMD(L)10,…) from all available and relevant studies on the different human health endpoints where test item related adverse effect is observed (and thus, when hazard is identified).

    In all the studies that have been carried out on 2,2-dimethyl-1,3-dioxolan-4-ylmethanol, except for eye irritation, no adverse effects have been observed, except in two studies for toxicity to reproduction (developmental toxicity). These two studies have been carried out in rabbits: a prenatal developmental toxicity study performed in 2022 (OECD 414, GLP, K.1) and an investigative prenatal developmental toxicity study performed in 2023/2024 (based on OECD 414, GLP, K.1). 

     

     

    Test item related adverse effects observed:

    In the first study (OECD 414) a general trend to reduction of 31% of food consumption between GD 7-9 which returned to normal on GD 22-23, was observed at the highest dose of 1000 mg/kg/d (and only at this dose level). This effect was considered as adverse for the following reasons:

    - The amplitude of the reduction of food consumption (down to -31% vs. controls)

    - The length of this period (from DG7-8 to GD9-10, which corresponds to the period of ossification hypersensitivity)

    - The association with dose-related delays in ossification (variations) and few soft-tissue malformations observed at 1000 mg/kg/d.

    So even if there is no associated body weight decrease, there are several congruent elements to consider this effect as adverse in this study. 

     

    As explained in the justification for classification, the first study raised suspicions of a possible developmental effect which required further investigation. To this end, a second investigative prenatal developmental toxicity study was launched in rabbits in 2023 (based on OECD 414, GLP, K.1) at a dose level of 1000 mg/kg/d with an increase of statistical power (555 pups were exposed to 1000 mg/kg/d in this 2nd study against 198 in the 1st one). 

    While the concordance of these elements is the most important point to support the conclusion of adversity of the effect observed in the first study, this concordance was partially invalidated with the investigative study. In fact, this second study shows no dose-related delays in ossification (variations), nor any malformation considered as adverse or substance-related.

    However, the second study confirms an effect on food consumption even more pronounced than in the 1st study, and also reflects a global trend (it is not based on isolated cases). This effect was considered adverse based on:

    - The large amplitude for the reduction of food consumption (down to a mean of -68% vs. controls).

    - The length of this period (from DG6 to GD12).

    - The association with slight body weight losses for which a test item-relationship cannot be excluded.

    So there are several congruent elements to consider this effect as adverse in this study per se, even if these elements are not exactly identical to what was identified in the first study. However, this second study allows us to conclude that this adverse effect is limited to a reduction in food consumption and a possible slight body weight loss and thus, of low severity. 

     

     

    Assessment of their relevance for risk assessment:

    As recommended in ECHA technical guidance [1], for DNEL derivation we have to select the correct and relevant starting point (dose-descriptor) to which we will apply assessment factors. The starting point is therefore the basis of DNEL and should be chosen carefully based on expert judgment. Work was therefore done to assess the relevance of the NOAELs identified in these two reproductive toxicity studies carried out in rabbits.

    In the two studies we observed a substance-related adverse effect at the highest dose administered, allowing the determination of a NOAEL. As mentioned by ECETOC in its technical report for the integration of human and animal data in chemical risk assessment [2], “what is important to the risk assessor is not only the quality of the study per se, but also the quality of the information provided to the reviewer of the study”. Thus not only the intrinsic relevance of the adverse effect observed was considered in this assessment, but also the relevance and usefulness of this adverse effect for DNEL derivation and risk assessment purposes. 

     

    According to ECETOC [3], “a slight but statistically significant reduction in body weight could plausibly be related to unpleasant taste in feeding”. In the case of the investigative study in rabbits the administration of the substance is done by gavage. It is however still a realistic hypothesis because during gavage, there may be residue of substance on the device used, and the animal may be subject to regurgitations. Taste of the test item could therefore still have an influence on food consumption. 

    Moreover, the rabbit is well known to be a stress-sensitive species and to be easily subject to reduced feeding in toxicological studies [4] [5]. In addition, rabbits are particularly sensitive to diet form and taste [5]. 

    In addition, the hypothesis of unpleasant taste is concordant with the results because the reduction in food consumption is first observed at GD6-7, just after starting the administration of the treatment (at GD6). Moreover, food consumption and body weight are linked because they were observed in the same period of time, and the correlation between these effects is well known. Therefore the reduction of food consumption helps to explain the slight body weight losses and to exclude other possible origins. 

    In the ECETOC technical report [3], this observation was cited as an example of low severity of effect to be considered for risk assessment and DNEL derivation. 

     

    According to WHO [6], an adverse effect is defined as a “change in the morphology, physiology, growth, development, reproduction, or life span of an organism, system, or (sub)population that results in an impairment of functional capacity, an impairment of the capacity to compensate for additional stress, or an increase in susceptibility to other influences”. As explained above, the effects observed are limited to food consumption and body weight decrease. Any association with other potential effects (delays in ossification and few soft-tissue malformations) was negated by the investigative study. The impairment of functional capacity or other influences is thus limited.

    ECETOC published [7] a series of considerations to assess the severity of an observed effect. Many of them agree with the low severity of the ones observed in the two rabbit studies:

    - There is no alteration in the general function of the test organism (or organ or tissue).

    - The effect disappears during the course of treatment. The reduction of food consumption and even more the slight body weight losses are transient and fully return towards control values during the study. 

    - The effect on food consumption and body weight are well known to be in correlation and to be fully reversible.

     

    In addition, several publications in the public literature are useful to understand that the effect on food consumption in rabbits is not a major concern. Several studies prove that feed restriction during pregnancy in rabbits can take place without any effect on reproduction and on the offspring [8] [9]. Concerning the maternal toxicity, pregnant rabbits are known to be especially sensitive to gastrointestinal disturbances and may foremost suffer enteropathy or pregnancy toxemia, a nutritional disorder that occurs secondary to insufficient food intake and metabolic effects [5]. None of these effects were observed in the investigative study in rabbits. The effect on food consumption observed in the investigative study is therefore not correlated with any known adverse effect in rabbits due to weight loss, which supports the idea of ââan effect of low concern.

     

    It can therefore be concluded that the effect on food consumption and body weight decrease, although identified as adverse, is of low severity and plausibly correlated with an alteration of taste when feeding. This type of effect is thus considered meaningless for human risk assessment purposes. Moreover it may not have much value in risk assessment due to the low potential impact on functional capacity. 



    Correlation with other studies and global view for risk assessment:

    According to ECHA technical guidance [1], the other aspect of adequacy is consistency. A weight-of-evidence approach should be used in assessing the level of consistency of the total data base and especially of the starting point (e.g. NOAEL in this case) to be brought forward to the risk characterisation. This approach requires a critical evaluation of the entire body of available data for consistency and biological plausibility.

    Other studies with adequate quality for risk assessment are available in this reach dossier. Only repeated dose toxicity studies and toxicity to reproduction were considered (there is no carcinogenicity study available and acute toxicity, skin sensitization, irritation/corrosion, genetic toxicity studies were considered not relevant to find a correlation with the adverse effect observed in rabbits).

    The available studies are:

    • Combined repeated dose toxicity and reproduction / developmental toxicity screening test in rats (OECD 422 – 2013)
    • Prenatal developmental toxicity in rats (OECD 414 – 2018)
    • Extended one-generation reproductive toxicity in rats (OECD 443 – 2023)
    • Dose-Range Finding Study & prenatal developmental toxicity in rabbits (OECD 414 – 2021/2022)
    • Investigative mechanistic study in rabbits based on OECD 414 – prenatal developmental toxicity (2023) 
    • Dose range finding study 14 days & sub-chronic inhalation toxicity study in rats – 90 days (OECD 413 – 2018)

    None of these studies shows any test item related adverse effect allowing the definition of a NOAEC and LOAEC, except in the two prenatal developmental toxicity studies in rabbits (as discussed previously). 

    Reliable inhalation study is available in rat. In this inhalation study (either in the DRF study or the main sub-chronic study) there was no treatment-related adverse effect at all, including in the body weight, body weight change or food consumption data up to the highest concentration of 5 mg/L. Therefore, the dossier contains reliable data to exclude the relevance of inhalation route for risk assessment. 

    Moreover, considering that there is no potential exposure to the substance via oral route, this conclusion gives more weight to consider a very low biological plausibility for the transposition of the identified oral adverse effect in rabbits (reduction of food consumption associated to body weight losses) to human health. 

    Therefore, according to all the data available in this REACH registration dossier, except for eye irritation, the only adverse effect observed is of very low concern and not relevant for human risk assessment. It is considered that no hazard is identified, except eye irritation. Thus no DNEL needs to be derived in this registration dossier.

    Additional information - General Population

    The registered substance is classified as Eye irritant 2A, H319. It is not possible to derive a DNEL based on the available data. According to the ECHA guidance on Information Requirements and Chemical Safety Assessment, Part E, Table E. 3-1 (Version 3.0 November 2016), the substance is categorized in the ‘low hazard’ band for eye irritation and a qualitative risk characterisation should be performed for this endpoint. In order to guarantee ‘adequately control of risks’, it is necessary to stipulate risk management measures that prevent exposure that will cause eye irritation.
