    Additional information - workers

    Short-term exposure - systemic effects

    Since n-BMA is of low acute toxicity with the median lethal dose values (LD50) being significantly greater than 2000 mg/kg by oral and dermal routes and an ALC of 4901 ppm (29000 mg/m³; mixed exposure to vapour/aerosol) by inhalation, the dermal and inhalation DNELs derived for long-term exposure are considered sufficiently protective of acute exposure. 

    Long-term exposure (inhalation) - systemic effects

    Based on an integrative assessment of nine available repeated dose studies with rats and mice, ranging from subacute to chronic exposure periods, a consensus NOAEC for systemic effects of 500 ppm can be derived for chronic exposure.

    Long-term exposure (inhalation) - local effects

    Animal data

    Based on a Weight-of-Evidence assessment of nine available repeated dose studies with rats and mice, ranging from subacute to chronic exposure periods, a consensus NOAEC for local effects of ca. 60 ppm can be derived for chronic exposure.

    Toxicokinetic and anatomical considerations on the interspecies AF

    Methacrylate esters are rapidly absorbed and metabolised to the corrosive methacrylic acid and the respective alcohol before further rapid metabolic steps (see toxicokinetics). Thus, it is considered as likely that interim presence of methacrylic acid contributes to the local irritative effects seen frequently in inhalation studies with volatile methacrylic esters. By analogy to the close structural analogue, MMA, the pattern of the critical effects of inhalation of n-BMA in animal studies (i.e. the olfactory epithelium being affected at lowest concentration) is consistent with toxicity resulting from metabolism of the inhaled material in the olfactory tissue by carboxylic esterases to methacrylic acid. Based on limited data from human tissue samples (that may not have been morphologically normal taken at polyp biopsy) with MMA, the activity of alpha-naphthylbutyrate carboxylesterase in human nasal respiratory tissue is less than that in the rat (Mattes & Mattes, 1992). In addition, rodents are obligate nose breathers, whereas humans can also breathe through their mouth, which is expected to reduce exposure of the nasal epithelium. There are also differing nasal flow patterns, with the greater airflow across the human olfactory epithelium during the expiratory phase when the vapour concentration would be considerably reduced as a result of absorption in the lower respiratory tract. Furthermore, there are significant morphological differences between species in the structure of the nasal cavity, which result in differences in concentrations of inhaled materials at the nasal tissue. These are reflected in differences in surface area normalised to minute ventilation, being fivefold greater in rodents than in humans (DeSesso, 1993). A much greater percentage of the nasal cavity is lined by olfactory epithelium in rats than in humans. From this perspective, an interspecies factor of 1 is considered to be sufficiently conservative.

     Acute, short-term exposure - local effects - inhalation

    The critical health effect is irritation in the upper respiratory tract (olfactory epithelium). The chronic DNEL will be protective against this endpoint.

     

     Long-term exposure - systemic effects - dermal exposure

    In absence of dermal studies, an oral study was used as POD for dermal DNELs. In a subchronic 90-day gavage study in rats with n-BMA, relevant signs of general systemic toxicity (limited to including effects on the liver activity (increased liver weight, prolonged prothrombin time, lower serum globulin and triglyceride levels in males and/or females) and kidneys weight (increased absolute weight in females) was observed at higher doses. Lesions in the olfactory region of the nose that were observed in this oral study are considered as irrelevant dosing artefact considering results of a  mechanistic study using i.p. dosing for systemic exposure without respiratory organ contact that do not show these effects (ERBC 2021). The NOAEL for toxicologically relevant signs of general systemic toxicity was 120 mg/kg body weight/day in both males and females.

    Acute, short-term and long-term exposure - local effects - dermal

    An induction-specific DNEL was derived for skin sensitization based on the EC3 value from a LLNA study (Harlan CCR 2013). The sensitisation threshold (EC3 value) was reported to be 43.6% w/v, indicative of a sensitiser of moderate potency. According to the Guidance on information requirements and chemical safety assessment, Chapter R.8, the EC3 value (in µg/cm²; here 10,900 µg/cm2= 10.9 mg/cm2 or 1.09 µg/m2) can be used as a surrogate for the LOAEL for induction. EC3 data generally correlate well with human skin sensitisation thresholds. However, there are cases where this correlation is poor and the two values may differ by 10-fold or more. Therefore, using a typical dose-response AF of 3 multiplied with a default AF of 5 for interspecies variation, the acute dermal DNEL was calculated to be 0.73 mg/cm² or 0.07 µg/m².
