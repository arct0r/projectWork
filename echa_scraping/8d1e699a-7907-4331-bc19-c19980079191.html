    Explanation for the modification of the dose descriptor starting point

    The long-term DNEL for systemic effects via the inhalation route is determined on the basis of the 180-day oral repeated dose toxicity study in rat (Smith, 1973), in which the NOAEL was determined to be ≥ 159 mg/kg bw/day.

    To correct the interspecies difference between rat and human the no observed effect level has to be corrected as follows:

    Corrected starting point for the inhalative route for workers:

    = NOAEL(oral) * (1/0.38 m³/kg bw/day) * (ABSoral-rat/ABSinh-human) * 6.7 m³ (8h) /10 m³ (8h)

    = 159 mg/kg bw/day * (1/0.38 m³/kg bw/day) * (1/2) * 0.67 m³ = 140.2 mg/m³

    (ABSoral-rat = oral absorption in rats, ABSinh-human = inhalation absorption rate in humans)

    Additional information - workers

    Workers might be exposed to silicic acid, potassium salt during manufacture, processing or filling in appropriate containers.

    The dermal as well as the inhalation DNEL for workers were determined using route-to-route extrapolation.

    The existing OEL (TRGS 900, June 2008) for dust is 3 mg/m3 (alveolar fraction) and 10 mg/m3 (respirable fraction). The more critical value of 3 mg/m3 is based on effects (5% elevation of the prevalence for chronic bronchitis and lung emphysema) observed in epidemiological studies taking into account a typical dust density at the workplace of 2.5 mg/cm3.

    The derived DNEL for inhalation is higher than the existing OEL for dust, therefore long-term systemic effects caused by potassium silicate are not expected to occur as long as the OEL is complied with. However, because of the high alkalinity of potassium silicate (pH values of the concentrated products being usually between 10 and 13), local effects to the skin, eyes and respiratory tract have to be accounted for.

    Additional information - General Population

    The direct and indirect skin contact, inhalation and oral routes as well as the accidental overexposure (eye or oral exposure) were identified and assessed by HERA (2005) as relevant consumer exposure scenarios for Silicic acid, potassium salt.

    The primary exposure results from dermal contact. Short-term exposure to dust may occur by the use of products in powder/granular form only, other application forms are of no concern with respect to inhalative exposure, expect when used in spray applications. Oral exposure for consumers usually plays a minor role.
